'Var-CNN 300\n4.7 Conﬁdence threshold\nAs the ﬁnal step of our attack, we apply a post-training\nthreshold on the softmax probability output of the net-\nwork (indicated by the “TH” block in Figure 5). If the\noutput class probability is less than this threshold, we\nchange the predicted class to the unmonitored class.\nIntuitively, this can be explained as deﬁning a certain\nminimum bound on model certainty before classifying\na sample. If a model is not certain about its classiﬁca-\ntion as a monitored website, we assume that the testing\ninput was really an unmonitored website that only par-\ntially matched a monitored site, and we classify it as\nunmonitored.\nThe threshold constraint also allows for direct con-\ntrol over TPR and FPR trade-oﬀ. Additional\nschemes such as classify-verify and adjusting the num-\nber of nearest neighbors require re-training to adjust\ntrade-oﬀs.\nIn contrast, Var-CNN allows us to easily adjust\nthresholds post-training. Moreover,\nwe can parallelize the training procedure to decrease\nruntimes. Since Var-CNN direction and Var-CNN time\nare independent models ensembled after training, they\ncan be trained separately on two GPUs. In addition, re-\ncent work in Deep Learning research has shown that\nmodels can be massively parallelized by using larger\nbatch sizes, special learning rate schemes, and more\nGPUs. A prime example of this is Goyal et al. [14], who\ntrained a ResNet-50 on a very large image classiﬁcation\ndataset in just one hour. Previously, it would have taken\nseveral days to train on a single GPU.\n6.2 Why does Var-CNN work in low-data\nsettings?\nAs noted in §5.2.3 and §5.2.4, Var-CNN’s accuracy im-\nprovements over DF increase in the following scenarios:'

'Var-CNN 305\n1. A ﬁxed number of monitored sites and a decreasing\nnumber of monitored traces in the closed-world.\n2. A ﬁxed number of monitored traces and an increas-\ning number of monitored sites in the closed-world.\n3. A decreasing number of monitored traces and un-\nmonitored training sites in the open-world.\nInthissection,weprovideapossiblegeneralexplanation\nfor these trends and relate them to the model techniques\nused in Var-CNN.\nFor the following discussion, consider neural net-\nwork optimization as ﬁnding a good hypothesis (i.e.,\none that achieves low training and generalization errors)\namong the overall hypothesis space. All of the above\nsettings make the hypothesis space more vast and am-\nbiguous to traverse. For instance, decreasing the num-\nber of monitored traces and unmonitored train sites\n(i.e., reducing the training data available to the clas-\nsiﬁer) provides less information for the optimizer to ﬁnd\na good hypothesis with low generalization error. On the\nother hand, increasing the number of monitored sites as\nin the second scenario expands the overall size of the\nhypothesis space with no additional optimization help\nfrom more monitored traces. As the hypothesis space\nchanges, a modern convolutional neural network with\nenoughweightparameterswouldstilllikelyachievegood\ntraining accuracy, as shown by Zhang et al. [48]. How-\never, as the optimization landscape is more vast and\nambiguous to traverse, it would have an increasingly dif-\nﬁcult time ﬁnding hypotheses that don’t overﬁt to the\ntraining data (i.e., that have low generalization error).\nIn contrast, one of the key beneﬁts of ensembles, as\nnoted by seminal work [10], is statistical. When the hy-\npothesis space becomes unclear as with the above sce-\nnarios, ensembles help ﬁlter out bad hypotheses that\noverﬁt to the training data, making the overall model\nhave better generalization capabilities. Given that Var-\nCNN is composed of two types of ensembles, one with\nan in-training combination of a dilated causal ResNet-\n18 and cumulative features and the other with a post-\ntraining ensemble of direction and timing models, we\nsuspect that these ensembles enable Var-CNN to per-\nform especially well when the optimization landscape\ngets more complex to navigate. Empirically, this was\nalso observed in §5.2.1, where the gap between the en-\nsemble model and the best performing constituent in-\ncreases as the number of monitored traces decreases.7 Future work\nAlthough Var-CNN outperforms prior WF attacks in\nevery setting tested, especially with small amounts of\ntraining data, there are still many directions where it\ncould improve. In this section, we discuss limitations of\nour work and possible avenues for future work.\nMore powerful baseline models. Since deep\nlearning is a rapidly accelerating ﬁeld [28], applications\nof new model architecture breakthroughs could lead to\nbetter results. For instance, here, we used the ResNet\narchitecture as our baseline CNN since it is currently\nthe most widely used state-of-the-art Image Classiﬁca-\ntion CNN. There are other Image Classiﬁcation models\nsuchaslargervariantsofResNetsorDenseNets[21]that\ncould give better results. In our preliminary tests with\nthese architectures, however, we did not see signiﬁcant-\nenoughaccuracyimprovementstojustifytheirincreased\ncomputational costs.\nIn addition, recent work on Synthetic Gradients [23]\ncould lead to RNNs with the ability to train on much\nlonger inputs (e.g., the packet sequences used in this\nwork). Since RNNs were speciﬁcally made for tempo-\nral sequences, this model might understand long-term\npacket interactions better than dilated causal convolu-\ntions.\nRegardless of the architecture choice, we would like\nto note that nearly all of the packet sequence classi-\nﬁcation insights used in this paper are architecture-\nindependent and can thus be applied to most future\ndeep learning attacks. For instance, dilated causal con-\nvolutions work with any CNN architecture and ensem-\nbleswithcumulativefeaturesandtimingdataworkwith\nnearly all neural network models.\nData augmentation. A common technique in\nComputer Vision research is to artiﬁcially expand\nthe training data size by using data augmentation—\ncropping, rotating, ﬂipping, shifting, and rescaling the\nimage. This technique works in Computer Vision be-\ncause the artiﬁcial data is often similar-enough to real-\nworld data that it is useful to the model. Similar tech-\nniques for packet sequences, such as shifting them a ran-\ndom number of packets one way or the other, could be\nused to achieve even better low-data performance.\nUser-sourced datasets. For instance, in a closed-world\nwith 100 sites and 90 monitored traces, both the di-\nrection and time models achieved above 90% accuracy\nwhile the metadata model consistently got 35% accu-\nracy. If we just averaged all three models post-training,\nthis caused an accuracy decrease since the metadata\nmodel was signiﬁcantly worse than the other two. This\nﬁnding led us to use metadata during training rather\nthan after. In our tests, we noticed that adding meta-\ndata to both the direction and time ResNets acted as\na stabilizer, reducing stochasticity and improving ﬁnal\naccuracy. In early experiments, we tried to use in-training\nweight-sharing techniques. While this certainly did not\ndouble the number of model parameters, it resulted in\nan accuracy drop compared to the individual direction\nand timing models.
